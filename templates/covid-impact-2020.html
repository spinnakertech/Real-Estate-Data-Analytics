Presentation & Charts

The national real estate markets performed relatively consistently in 2018 and 2019, then took a dramatic and sudden downturn in 2020, most notably in April and May.

The chart below shows Homes Sold, New Listings, and Pending Sales for the past three years. The numbers displayed are "year-over-year" differences in those data points.

Data provided by Redfin, a national real estate brokerage.
Data provided by United States Bureau of Labor Statistics.

Such a dramatic and sudden downturn in the real estate market may be due to economic factors such as Unemployment, which historically is linked to real estate. Historically, high unemployment leads to lower home sales

In April and May 2020, the unemployment rate took a similar dramatic and sudden spike as the Real Estate market. The unemployment rate remained high for the rest of the year, however, but the housing market seemed to bounce back after only two months.

In late January 2020, the COVID-19 Coronavirus pandemic hit; and it was spreading in communities by February 2020. In response to rising COVID rates, the federal government issued a national state of emergency and the states began implementing Stay-at-Home orders.

Data provided by United States Department of Health & Human Services.

These Stay-at-Home Orders required residents to stay home, except to engage in essential functions, such as shopping for groceries. Essential businesses were exempt from these orders.

Some real estate services were considered essential businesses and theoretically could continue to operate. Individuals, however, were not permitted to leave their houses and home sellers did not want strangers in their homes.

This resulted in sellers pulling their homes off the market and buyers staying home instead of shopping for homes.

Data provided by Redfin, a national real estate brokerage.

The Real Estate markets crashed in April and May 2020. In June, however, they came roaring back!

This dramatic and sudden resurgence can be attributed to the states lifting their Stay-at-Home Orders, most of which were lifted in May.

Data provided by United States Department of Health & Human Services.
